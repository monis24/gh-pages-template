US Passport Number

US passport numbers are unique identifiers assigned to each passport issued by the United States government. Valid passport numbers typically consist of nine characters, either all numeric or a combination of one letter followed by eight digits. For example, A12345678, B98765432, and C00000000 are all valid passport numbers. The passport ID system also recognizes numbers like D11111111 and E99999999 as legitimate identifiers.

Other examples of valid US passport numbers include F01234567, G98765432, H55555555, and I87654321. It's worth noting that while most modern US passport numbers begin with a letter, some older passports may have all-numeric identifiers, such as 123456789, which is also considered valid.

Invalid US passport numbers can take various forms, often deviating from the standard nine-character format. For instance, A1234567 and E9999999 are too short, while B987654321 and I876543210 are too long to be valid passport numbers. The passport number system also rejects identifiers that don't follow the correct alphanumeric pattern, such as c12345678 (lowercase first letter) or D1234567A (letter at the end).

Other examples of invalid passport numbers include those with incorrect formatting or characters. For example, F0123456A has a letter in the wrong position, 1G8765432 starts with a number instead of a letter, H5555555 is missing a digit, and J-12345678 contains a hyphen, which is not allowed in US passport numbers. These formats do not comply with the US passport number standards and should not be recognized as valid identifiers.
