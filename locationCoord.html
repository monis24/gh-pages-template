Location Coordinates

Positive Test Cases

GPS coordinates are essential for precise location identification. Common formats include latitude and longitude pairs such as 40.7128° N, 74.0060° W and 51.5074N, 0.1278W. These coordinates are crucial for geolocation services and mapping applications, providing exact positions on the Earth's surface.

Coordinate systems can represent locations in various formats. For example, 35.6762, 139.6503 and +40.7128, -74.0060 use decimal degrees without directional indicators. Other valid representations include 0 N, 0 E (the prime meridian), 45.4215° N, 75.6972° W, S 89.9999, W 179.9999 (near the South Pole), and 37° N, 115° W. These geopoints demonstrate the flexibility of coordinate notation while maintaining accuracy.

Incorrect or invalid coordinate formats can cause issues in mapping and navigation systems. Examples of such invalid formats include 90.1234° W, 180.5678° N and North 40°, East 74°, which do not conform to standard latitude and longitude ranges or formats. These invalid coordinates would be rejected by most geolocation systems.

Similarly, non-standard or incomplete coordinate representations can lead to errors in location services. Instances like N90', E180' and 45.4215E°, 75.6972S° are invalid. More complex examples such as 37°14'06.4"N, 115°48'40.1"W, 1000m, South 12.3456, East 78.9012, and 51°30'26.0"N, 0°07'39.0"W, London further illustrate potential issues with non-standard coordinate representations in geographic contexts.
