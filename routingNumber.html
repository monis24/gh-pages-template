ABA Routing Number

Formatted (High Confidence Level)

ABA routing numbers, also known as routing transit numbers (RTN), are crucial identifiers in the American banking system. Properly formatted ABA numbers typically appear with hyphens separating the digits into groups. Valid examples include 0123-4567-8, 1234-5678-9, and 2345-6789-0. These formatted numbers provide a high level of confidence in identifying legitimate routing numbers used by financial institutions.

Other valid formats for ABA routing numbers include 3012-3456-7, 6789-0123-4, and 7012-3456-7. Additionally, some systems may represent the numbers with a single hyphen, such as 01234567-8, 12345678-9, and 23456789-0. All these formats are recognized as valid representations of ABA routing numbers by the American Banking Association.

Invalid ABA routing numbers often have incorrect digit patterns or formatting. For instance, 1323-4567-8 and 3345-6789-0 do not follow the proper numerical sequence for valid routing numbers. Similarly, numbers starting with 4, 5, or 9, such as 4012-3456-7, 5789-0123-4, and 9012-3456-7, are not used in the ABA routing system.

Other examples of invalid formatted ABA numbers include those with incorrect groupings like 0123-456-78 or 1234-5678-90. Numbers that are too short (2345-6789) or contain non-numeric characters (3012-3456-7A) are also invalid. These formats do not comply with the ABA routing number standards and should not be recognized as valid identifiers.

Unformatted (High to Low Confidence)

Unformatted ABA routing numbers appear as a continuous string of nine digits without any separators. Valid examples include 012345678, 123456789, and 234567890. These numbers, while lacking hyphens, still represent legitimate routing transit numbers used in the American banking system. Other valid unformatted ABA numbers are 301234567, 678901234, and 701234567.

Additional instances of valid unformatted ABA routing numbers include 801234567, 012345678 (repeated from above), 123456789 (repeated), and 234567890 (repeated). While these numbers lack the clear formatting of hyphenated versions, they still contain the necessary nine digits to represent full ABA routing numbers used by financial institutions.
